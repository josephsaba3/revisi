# Brand Voice Rubric

Use this rubric to judge copy as a practical business asset.

## Brand Fit

Good: The copy sounds like the brand could actually say it. It matches the supplied voice guide or the strongest confirmed source copy. Tone, word choice, confidence level, humor, warmth, and directness feel consistent.

Bad: The copy sounds imported from a generic template. It shifts between founder-led, corporate, playful, clinical, and salesy without intent.

Look for: tone consistency, audience fit, emotional register, level of polish, words the brand would or would not say.

## Audience Fit

Good: The copy speaks to the right reader, their current situation, their level of knowledge, and the questions they need answered before moving forward.

Bad: The copy addresses a generic audience, assumes the wrong level of sophistication, misses the reader's real anxiety, or talks to the business instead of the buyer.

Look for: reader specificity, buying-stage fit, industry familiarity, emotional context, sophistication level, practical objections, language the audience would recognize.

## Clarity

Good: A new reader can quickly understand what the product/service is, who it is for, what problem it solves, and what to do next.

Bad: The page explains outcomes without explaining the thing being sold. It uses abstractions before concrete nouns.

Look for: clear offer, concrete product category, simple explanation, visible next step, no buried value proposition.

## Human Sound

Good: The writing feels like a competent person talking to a real reader. It has rhythm, specificity, and useful edges.

Bad: The writing feels synthetic, over-balanced, overly symmetrical, stuffed with transitions, or afraid to make a plain claim.

Look for: natural cadence, varied sentence length, restrained adjectives, fewer formulaic phrases, fewer em dashes.

## Specificity

Good: Claims are grounded in real features, use cases, constraints, workflows, examples, numbers, screenshots, customer situations, or before/after states.

Bad: Claims could fit any competitor. Benefits stay at the level of `save time`, `streamline workflow`, or `unlock growth`.

Look for: named audience, concrete tasks, tangible outcomes, credible details, product mechanics.

## Trust

Good: Readers get reasons to believe the claims: proof, constraints, examples, transparent tradeoffs, clear process, social proof, or honest limits.

Bad: Big claims appear without evidence. The copy hides caveats or leans on hype.

Look for: proof points, screenshots, demos, plain pricing language, examples, named mechanisms, absence of fake certainty.

## Conversion Readiness

Good: The copy guides a reader from problem to fit to action. CTAs match buying intent and reduce uncertainty.

Bad: CTAs are weak, generic, hidden, or disconnected from the reader's next question.

Look for: CTA clarity, objection handling, offer hierarchy, friction reduction, section sequencing.

Use Conversion Readiness as a contextual modifier, not a default core metric, unless the content is explicitly a landing page, product page, service page, sales page, or signup page.

## Distinctiveness

Good: The copy has memorable choices: a sharp point of view, category contrast, unusual specificity, founder perspective, or a phrase the reader would remember.

Bad: The copy sounds interchangeable with dozens of companies.

Look for: point of view, proprietary language, concrete tradeoffs, category stance, memorable phrasing.

## AI Sludge Risk

High risk writing is generic, over-polished, pattern-heavy, and low-specificity. It may be technically fluent but emotionally flat. Treat AI Sludge Risk as a penalty signal, not a quality dimension.
